Why Losing Them Hurts More Than
Finding Someone New Feels Good

You've met someone genuinely great since the breakup. They're kind, they're interested, they make sense. But somehow the person you lost still takes up more space. Your brain isn't broken. It's doing the math wrong on purpose.

✦ LUMI TAROT Neuroscience × Love Series #4

Rationally, you know the relationship wasn't perfect. You know there were real problems. You might even know, objectively, that the new person in your life is better for you in measurable ways. And yet the loss of the old one sits heavier. The grief outweighs the hope. The absence feels louder than the presence.

This asymmetry isn't a sign that you loved them more, or that this new person doesn't matter. It's a fundamental feature of how the human brain processes loss versus gain.

🧠 The Science

Loss Aversion

Kahneman & Tversky · Prospect Theory · 1979

Nobel Prize-winning research by Kahneman and Tversky demonstrated that losses feel approximately twice as powerful as equivalent gains. Losing $100 feels roughly twice as bad as finding $100 feels good. Your brain evolved this asymmetry as a survival mechanism — losses historically meant death, while gains were just nice. In relationships, this means the absence of someone who was once present hits neurologically harder than the presence of someone new.

Why You Keep Going Back to the Comparison

Loss aversion also explains why breakups produce such relentless mental comparison. Your brain keeps measuring what you had against what you have now — not to torture you, but because it's trying to calculate whether the loss was worth it, and whether there's a way to recover the asset.

This is the part that feels like hope but often isn't. The brain's loss-minimization system can masquerade as "maybe we should try again." It's worth asking whether you genuinely want them back, or whether your nervous system is just trying to undo the loss.

✦ Loss Aversion in Relationships Looks Like

• Comparing every new person unfavorably to the one you lost
• Remembering the relationship as better than it was — especially right after the breakup
• Feeling like you'd do anything to get it back, even if you didn't want it when you had it
• The new person's flaws feeling more significant than the ex's flaws ever did
• The grief feeling disproportionate to how good the relationship actually was

What the Tarot Reflects in This State

🃏 Cards That Carry Loss Energy

Five of Cups Four of Cups Six of Cups

Five of Cups is the classic loss card — standing over what was spilled, not noticing what's still standing. Four of Cups is emotional withdrawal, turning inward and away from what's being offered. Six of Cups is nostalgia — looking backward at something that felt simpler or sweeter, often revisiting the past rather than receiving the present.

Working With This, Not Against It

You can't override loss aversion with logic. Telling yourself "they weren't that great" rarely works because the brain knows you're trying to trick it. What does work is slowly building new experiences that have their own weight. The new relationship (or life, or chapter) needs time to accumulate its own memories, its own presence — until it starts to outweigh the absence rather than compete with it.

The other thing that helps: acknowledging the loss as real. Trying to skip the grief, or replace it too quickly, often extends it. The brain needs to process the loss as a loss — not rationalize it away — before it can genuinely move forward.
