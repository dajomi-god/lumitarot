Why the Almost-Relationship Feels
More Intense Than the Real One

You've had real relationships that ended and you moved on fine. But this situationship — the one that was never even official — has taken up more space in your head than any of them. That's not a coincidence.

✦ LUMI TAROT Neuroscience × Love Series #3

There's something uniquely consuming about the in-between. The person who texts at midnight but won't define things. The one who makes you feel everything but commits to nothing. The situation where you don't even know what you're grieving when it ends, because it was never officially anything.

And yet here you are, thinking about it more than you've thought about actual relationships. This is not a personal failure. This is dopamine doing exactly what dopamine was designed to do.

🧠 The Science

Intermittent Reinforcement & Dopamine Spikes

B.F. Skinner · Variable Reward Schedules · Dopamine System

Dopamine isn't released when you get a reward. It's released in anticipation of a possible reward. This is why slot machines are more addictive than guaranteed payouts — the uncertainty is the drug. When someone is inconsistent (warm, then cold; available, then gone), your brain's dopamine system goes into overdrive trying to predict the next reward. The uncertainty doesn't make them less appealing. It makes them neurologically addictive.

Why Stable Feels "Boring"

If you've ever met someone kind, consistent, and clearly interested — and felt almost nothing — this is why. A predictable reward doesn't spike dopamine the same way. Your brain registers it as safe, but not exciting. The nervous system that's been trained on hot-and-cold doesn't know what to do with steady warmth.

This creates a painful pattern: we mistake intensity for depth, and anxiety for attraction. The person who keeps you guessing feels like the real connection. The person who shows up reliably feels flat. Not because they are — but because your dopamine system has been calibrated to the wrong signal.

✦ Signs You're in the Dopamine Loop

• You feel more alive right after they text than during conversations with people who genuinely care about you
• You analyze their behavior obsessively, looking for patterns
• The good moments feel extraordinary — partly because they're rare
• You've had this exact dynamic before, with different people
• You tell yourself "I just need to know where this is going" but the not-knowing is also part of the pull

What the Cards See Here

🃏 Cards That Appear in This Dynamic

Seven of Cups The Moon The Devil

Seven of Cups is the card of illusion and projection — seeing what you want to see, building a story around possibility rather than reality. The Moon is the fog of uncertainty — nothing is clear, but instead of walking away, you stay and keep looking for clarity in the dark. The Devil is the attachment that feels impossible to break even when you know it's not serving you. The chains in the card are loose — they can be removed — but the figure doesn't move.

How to Recalibrate

The way out of the dopamine loop isn't willpower — it's neuroplasticity. Your brain learned this pattern, and it can learn a different one. But it requires deliberately interrupting the cycle enough times that the pattern loses its grip.

That means not checking their profile. Not reading the conversation for the fifteenth time. Not giving the brain the micro-hits of engagement that keep the loop running. Every time you don't do those things, you're weakening the neural pathway slightly. It's slow. But it works.

The deeper work is recalibrating what "attraction" feels like — learning to distinguish genuine connection from dopamine response. That takes time and often a different kind of relationship to compare against.
